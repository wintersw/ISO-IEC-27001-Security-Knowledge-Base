# Physical and Environmental Security

Physical and environmental security protects people, information, equipment, and supporting utilities against unauthorized access, damage, interference, and loss. Controls must reflect the site, threat, safety duties, occupancy model, and business impact—not only the type of lock installed.

## Layered design

- Define public, controlled, restricted, and high-security zones and the assets within them.
- Use boundaries, reception, badges, visitor sponsorship, alarms, and monitoring in proportion to risk.
- Protect offices, server rooms, cabling, loading areas, storage, and disposal routes.
- Address fire, water, temperature, humidity, power, fuel, and telecommunications dependencies.
- Position screens and equipment to reduce observation and tampering; apply clear-desk and clear-screen rules where justified.
- Control off-premises assets, remote work locations, removable media, maintenance, reuse, and secure disposal.

Privacy, labor law, accessibility, safety, and emergency egress constrain surveillance and access-control design. Recorded footage and entry logs are themselves sensitive information with defined purpose, retention, and access.

## Evidence and testing

Retain zone plans, access lists and reviews, visitor records, alarm and camera test results, environmental-monitoring trends, maintenance records, disposal certificates, incident records, and recovery tests for utility failures.

## Practical example

A shared office places network equipment in a locked monitored room, reviews badge access quarterly, records escorted visitors, uses water and temperature sensors, tests uninterruptible power supplies, and keeps emergency egress usable for everyone.

## Common mistakes

- assuming a cloud service removes all physical responsibilities
- retaining badge or video data indefinitely
- protecting the data center while ignoring offices, wiring closets, home working, and disposal
- installing security measures that obstruct emergency or accessible exit routes
