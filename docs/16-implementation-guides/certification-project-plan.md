# Certification Project Plan

Certification is an assurance milestone, not the project's security outcome. The plan should leave behind an information security management system (ISMS) that has accountable owners, representative operating evidence, known residual risks, and a sustainable calendar after the audit team leaves.

## Objectives and acceptance criteria

- define and approve a justified scope with interfaces and exclusions;
- establish processes that meet applicable requirements and the organization's risk decisions;
- demonstrate that selected controls operate across the complete intended population;
- close or formally govern findings, exceptions, and residual risks;
- complete independent internal audit and effective management review; and
- transfer the implementation programme into normal ISMS operation.

For each workstream, define an owner, dependencies, resources, output, acceptance criterion, evidence source, due date, risk, and operational handover owner. “Document written” or “tool installed” is rarely a sufficient acceptance criterion.

## Workstreams

- scope and context
- governance and roles
- risk management
- policies and procedures
- Annex A control implementation
- evidence collection
- internal audit
- management review
- corrective actions
- certification audit support

## Milestones

1. project kickoff
2. scope approved
3. methodology approved
4. first risk assessment completed
5. SoA approved
6. priority controls implemented
7. internal audit completed
8. management review completed
9. corrective actions closed
10. certification audit

11. operational handover and post-certification review

## Best practices

- Build evidence throughout the project.
- Do not schedule certification before the information security management system (ISMS) has operated.
- Run a readiness review before Stage 1.
- Treat Stage 1 feedback seriously.
- Keep project risks separate from information-security risks and link both where one affects the other.
- Use several smaller evidence and acceptance reviews instead of one late readiness exercise.
- Plan competence, recurring calendars, supplier dependencies, audit independence, and evidence retention before closure.
- Treat scope, timing, resource, and control-design changes as governed decisions with risk impact.


## Practical example

A software provider creates workstreams for scope, risk, governance, controls, evidence, and assurance. Access review is not accepted when its procedure is approved; it is accepted after the identity population is reconciled, reviewers complete a representative cycle, removals are verified, exceptions are governed, and the future process owner accepts the calendar and evidence repository. The certification date remains conditional on sufficient operating evidence and closure of readiness findings.

## Evidence to retain

Retain records showing both design decisions and actual operation, such as:

- approved plan and scope
- milestones, owners, and dependencies
- completed work records
- review results and accepted deviations

Intent documents are insufficient on their own; retain scoped operating records, approvals, exceptions, and verified follow-up.
