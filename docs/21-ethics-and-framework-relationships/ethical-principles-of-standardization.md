# Ethical Principles of Standardization

Standardization affects markets, suppliers, customers, employees, and society. Ethical standardization should therefore be open, transparent, balanced, and relevant.

## Core principles

| Principle | Meaning for information security management system (ISMS) adoption |
|---|---|
| Transparency | Requirements, decisions, and evidence expectations are visible |
| Openness | Affected stakeholders can contribute where appropriate |
| Impartiality | No group receives unjustified privilege |
| Consensus | Decisions are discussed and justified |
| Relevance | Controls address real risks and business needs |
| Coherence | Frameworks and policies do not contradict each other |
| Due care | Decisions are made carefully and documented |
| Respect | Meetings and reviews include diverse views |

## Applying these principles internally

Organizations can mirror these principles when adopting ISO/IEC 27001:

- include affected teams in policy design
- document scope and applicability decisions
- explain control selection
- allow controlled exceptions
- avoid anti-competitive or supplier-biased requirements
- review whether requirements remain relevant
- ensure security committees hear dissenting views

## Common mistakes

- adopting a standard only for marketing
- excluding affected staff from design
- using certification pressure to impose impractical rules
- equating formal compliance with ethical sufficiency
- ignoring supplier and user impacts

## Evidence

- stakeholder consultation
- policy review comments
- decision logs
- standard selection rationale
- management approval


## Practical example

Leadership compares this perspective with the organization's legal duties, values, risk appetite, and existing management systems before deciding which practices to adopt and how to communicate them.
