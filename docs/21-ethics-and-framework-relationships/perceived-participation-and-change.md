# Perceived Participation and Security Change

Perceived participation means people feel they had a meaningful opportunity to influence a change, even if not every preference was adopted.

## Why this matters

Employees are more likely to accept security measures when they understand the reason, are heard during design, and see that feedback affects implementation.

## Participation methods

- stakeholder interviews
- pilot groups
- policy review comments
- developer security guilds
- user experience testing
- exception feedback analysis
- post-rollout surveys
- office hours

## Rollout model

1. Explain the risk and objective.
2. Identify affected groups.
3. Gather feedback.
4. Test the process.
5. Communicate changes made from feedback.
6. Roll out with support.
7. Measure adoption and friction.
8. Improve.

## Warning signs

- employees bypass the control
- exceptions increase
- helpdesk tickets spike
- managers approve access without review
- teams claim security is “not realistic”
- survey scores decline

## Evidence

- feedback records
- pilot results
- communication plan
- survey results
- exception trends
- improvement actions


## Practical example

Leadership compares this perspective with the organization's legal duties, values, risk appetite, and existing management systems before deciding which practices to adopt and how to communicate them.
