# NIST CSF 2.0 Govern Function

The Govern Function establishes and monitors the organization's cybersecurity risk-management strategy, expectations, and policy. It does not replace technical controls. Instead, it creates the conditions that allow Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover activities to be prioritized and sustained.

## Governance outcomes in practice

The Govern Function can be translated into six practical responsibilities:

### Organizational context

Leadership understands the mission, stakeholders, critical services, contractual obligations, regulatory expectations, and dependencies that shape cybersecurity decisions.

### Risk-management strategy

The organization defines risk appetite, risk criteria, escalation thresholds, treatment expectations, and the relationship between cybersecurity risk and enterprise risk.

### Roles, responsibilities, and authorities

Decision rights are explicit. Risk owners, control owners, service owners, data owners, security leaders, and executive sponsors understand their responsibilities.

### Policy

Policies express the organization's security expectations and are supported by standards, procedures, workflows, training, and enforcement.

### Oversight

Governance forums review risk, performance, exceptions, incidents, assurance results, supplier risk, and improvement actions.

### Cybersecurity supply-chain risk management

Supplier and technology dependencies are considered before purchase, during operation, and at exit.

## Example

A cloud collaboration platform is widely used, but no business owner is assigned. The governance issue is not merely technical. The organization must assign ownership, define acceptable data, approve the risk, confirm supplier obligations, and decide how continued compliance will be monitored.

## Best practices

- Integrate cybersecurity reporting into existing board and executive governance.
- Define risk appetite in operational terms.
- Track management decisions until closure.
- Escalate resource constraints rather than hiding them in operational backlogs.
- Use assurance results to test whether policy expectations are being met.


## Evidence to retain

Retain records showing both design decisions and actual operation, such as:

- approved strategy or governance decision
- authority and accountability record
- risk and performance reporting
- oversight minutes and tracked actions

Intent documents are insufficient on their own; retain scoped operating records, approvals, exceptions, and verified follow-up.
