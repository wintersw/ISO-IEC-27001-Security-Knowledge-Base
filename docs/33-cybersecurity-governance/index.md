# Cybersecurity Governance

Cybersecurity governance determines how an organization sets direction, allocates authority, accepts risk, measures performance, and holds leaders accountable for cybersecurity outcomes.

ISO/IEC 27001 provides a management-system structure for leadership, risk, controls, assurance, and improvement. NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 complements that structure by organizing cybersecurity outcomes around six Functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. The addition of the Govern Function makes governance visible as the mechanism that shapes and prioritizes the other five Functions.

## Why governance needs its own chapter

Operational teams can implement controls, but they cannot independently resolve all of the decisions that shape security. Decisions about investment, risk tolerance, legacy technology, supplier dependency, product strategy, data use, and major remediation priorities belong to accountable governance.

A healthy governance model makes four things clear:

1. **Direction:** what security outcomes matter and why.
2. **Authority:** who can make which decisions.
3. **Assurance:** how leaders know the system is working.
4. **Accountability:** who owns unresolved risks and overdue actions.

## Practical example

A company has repeated critical vulnerabilities in a legacy customer platform. The security team can scan and report, but governance must decide whether to fund replacement, accept temporary risk, reduce service scope, or impose compensating controls. The decision should be documented, time-limited, linked to the risk register, and reviewed through management governance.
