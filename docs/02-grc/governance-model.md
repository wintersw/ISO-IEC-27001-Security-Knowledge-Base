# Security Governance Model

A governance model defines how security decisions are made, approved, monitored, and improved.

## Governance layers

| Layer | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Board / executive oversight | Direction, risk appetite, major decisions |
| Security steering committee | Priorities, resources, risk decisions |
| Risk and control owners | Day-to-day accountability |
| Security operations | Detection, response, technical control operation |
| Internal audit | Independent assurance |

## Decision types

- risk acceptance
- exception approval
- control prioritization
- security objectives
- budget and resource allocation
- policy approval
- corrective action escalation

## Best practices

- Define decision rights.
- Retain minutes and action logs.
- Use risk language rather than tool language.
- Escalate overdue actions.
- Review metrics that inform decisions.

## Governance operating mechanics

Define the forums and paths through which authority is exercised. Each forum should have a mandate, chair, membership, quorum or decision rule, delegated limits, input pack, conflict-of-interest rule, escalation path, minutes, action tracking, and review cadence. Avoid committees that can discuss risk but cannot make or route a decision.

### Challenge, appeal, and dispute

People need a safe way to challenge a security decision that is impractical, inconsistent, unsupported by evidence, or outside the decision-maker's authority. The path should identify:

- who can request reconsideration and within what period;
- which evidence and business impact must be supplied;
- who reviews the decision independently or at the next authority level;
- which temporary safeguards apply while the issue is unresolved; and
- how the final decision, rationale, dissent, expiry, and follow-up are retained.

An appeal is not an exception shortcut. If the final decision retains additional risk, the authorized risk-acceptance process still applies.

### Practical example

A product team disputes a security requirement that would delay a contractual release. The security architect documents the threat and options; the product owner documents customer and schedule impact. A delegated steering forum approves a time-limited compensating control, names the executive risk owner, sets an expiry date, and requires a post-release verification. The record shows both challenge and accountable decision-making.

## ISO requirement, implementation guidance, and best practice

- **ISO requirement:** This chapter explains **Security Governance Model** without reproducing standard text. Determine formal obligations from the applicable clauses, scope, risk treatment, Statement of Applicability, and binding legal or contractual requirements.
- **Implementation guidance:** Adapt the described roles, frequency, workflow, and evidence to the organization.
- **Best practice:** Enhancements are optional unless adopted through policy, contract, or risk treatment.

## Practical example

Leadership applies this topic during a quarterly governance review. Decision-makers compare risk exposure with objectives and obligations, approve actions, and record who is accountable for follow-up.

## Evidence to retain

Retain records showing both design decisions and actual operation, such as:

- meeting decision or approval record
- risk, obligation, or objective linkage
- assigned action and status
- follow-up review showing whether the decision was effective

Intent documents are insufficient on their own; retain scoped operating records, approvals, exceptions, and verified follow-up.
