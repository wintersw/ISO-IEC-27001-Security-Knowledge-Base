# Clause 5 — Leadership

Clause 5 ensures the information security management system (ISMS) is owned by the organization, not only by the security team. It requires top management to lead, approve policy, assign roles, and provide resources — not just sign off on documents.

## Why this matters

Without visible leadership, security becomes a delegated technical function with no authority to resolve competing priorities. When top management actively directs the ISMS, security decisions carry organizational weight, resources are committed, and accountability is clear. Auditors look for evidence that leadership is exercised, not merely documented.

## Key concepts

- **Leadership and commitment (5.1):** Top management must integrate ISMS requirements into business processes, communicate the importance of effective information security, ensure resources are available, and promote continual improvement. This is not delegation — it requires demonstrable action.
- **Information security policy (5.2):** A top-level document approved by management that states commitment to security, sets the framework for objectives, and commits to satisfying applicable requirements and continual improvement. It must be communicated and available as documented information.
- **Roles, responsibilities and authorities (5.3):** Top management must assign and communicate who is responsible for what within the ISMS. This includes authority to report on ISMS performance and to initiate corrective action.

## Practical implementation

1. **Secure top management sponsorship** with a business case that connects security to operational risk, customer expectations, regulatory exposure, and reputational impact.
2. **Establish a security steering committee** or equivalent forum where management-level decisions on risk, resources, and priorities are recorded.
3. **Draft and approve the information security policy** as a short, signed, publicly communicable document. Avoid burying it in technical language or excessive detail — reserve detail for supporting standards and procedures.
4. **Define ISMS roles** explicitly: who is the top management representative, who owns the ISMS, who owns risk decisions, and who has authority to stop unsafe activity. Document and communicate these assignments.
5. **Include security in regular business rhythms** — quarterly business reviews, project gate approvals, budget cycles — so it is not a parallel track that can be ignored.
6. **Retain evidence** of management decisions: signed policies, meeting minutes with actions, resource allocation records, and communications to the organization.
7. **Demonstrate continual commitment** through management review participation, resourcing decisions, and follow-through on improvement actions.

## Security-team best practices

- Present security risk in business terms: financial impact, service disruption, regulatory penalty, customer trust.
- Prepare a one-page leadership dashboard showing the handful of metrics that matter to executives.
- Escalate decisions that exceed the security team's authority immediately — do not absorb risk silently.
- Maintain a decision log that records who decided what, on what basis, and when review is due.
- When top management changes, re-establish the relationship and reaffirm ISMS commitments.

## Evidence

- approved and signed information security policy
- ISMS role assignment records (RACI or equivalent)
- management review meeting minutes showing active participation
- resource allocation decisions (budget, headcount, tools)
- communications from top management on security expectations
- security steering committee terms of reference and attendance records

## Common mistakes

- The policy is signed once and never revisited despite organizational change.
- Management review is attended by delegates who cannot make decisions.
- ISMS roles are defined on paper but the named individuals are unaware of their responsibilities.
- Resources are promised but never materialize; the security team absorbs the gap without escalation.
- Leadership commitment is only demonstrated during the audit cycle.

## ISO requirement, implementation guidance, and best practice

- **ISO requirement:** Clause 5 requires top management to demonstrate leadership and commitment, establish an information security policy, and assign organizational roles, responsibilities, and authorities. These are mandatory ISMS requirements.
- **Implementation guidance:** Define roles proportionate to organizational size. A small organization may combine responsibilities; document how segregation conflicts are managed.
- **Best practice:** Use a security steering committee with documented terms of reference and delegated authority limits. Link policy commitments to measurable objectives reported in management review.

## Practical example

A mid-sized SaaS company undergoing ISO 27001 certification identifies that its CEO has not been visibly involved. The CISO prepares a one-page briefing on the certification business case, the top three residual risks requiring executive decision, and the quarterly time commitment needed. The CEO approves the policy, attends the first steering committee, assigns a VP as the management representative with authority to make risk decisions up to a defined threshold, and records a quarterly security review in the board calendar. The signed policy, committee charter, role assignment memo, and board calendar entry become auditable evidence.
