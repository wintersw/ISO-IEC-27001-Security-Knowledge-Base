# Assets, Threats, Vulnerabilities, and Consequences

## Asset

Anything that has value to the organization or supports something of value. Assets include information, applications, services, infrastructure, people, facilities, suppliers, and reputation.

## Threat

A potential cause of an unwanted event. Threats include malicious actors, mistakes, hardware failure, utility disruption, fire, fraud, legal change, and supplier failure.

## Vulnerability

A weakness or condition that a threat can exploit or trigger. Examples include excessive privilege, missing patches, unclear ownership, unsupported software, weak contracts, and untested recovery procedures.

## Consequence

The business outcome if the risk materializes: regulatory exposure, financial loss, safety impact, service interruption, contractual breach, or reputational damage.

## Example

- Asset: customer identity service
- Threat: credential-stuffing attacker
- Vulnerability: no rate limiting and weak monitoring
- Event: automated account compromise
- Consequence: unauthorized disclosure and customer fraud

## ISO requirement, implementation guidance, and best practice

- **ISO requirement:** This chapter explains **Assets, Threats, Vulnerabilities, and Consequences** without reproducing standard text. Determine formal obligations from the applicable clauses, scope, risk treatment, Statement of Applicability, and binding legal or contractual requirements.
- **Implementation guidance:** Adapt the described roles, frequency, workflow, and evidence to the organization.
- **Best practice:** Enhancements are optional unless adopted through policy, contract, or risk treatment.

## Evidence to retain

Retain records showing both design decisions and actual operation, such as:

- documented design or risk decision
- implemented safeguard or configuration record
- test, review, or monitoring result showing the intended outcome

Intent documents are insufficient on their own; retain scoped operating records, approvals, exceptions, and verified follow-up.
