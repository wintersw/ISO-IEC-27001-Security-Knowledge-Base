# A.5.6 Contact with special interest groups

## Overview

Trusted professional groups can provide early warnings, peer knowledge, and specialist advice that an organization cannot efficiently develop alone. Participation should have a clear purpose and rules for protecting confidential information.

## Purpose

This control ensures that the organization maintains appropriate contact with special interest groups, security forums, and professional associations to stay informed about threats, vulnerabilities, and best practices. Isolation from the security community means learning about threats only after they have caused damage.

## ISO requirement, implementation guidance, and best practice

- **ISO requirement:** Annex A controls are not automatically mandatory. Determine necessary controls through risk treatment, record applicability in the Statement of Applicability, and implement A.5.6 when selected or otherwise committed to.
- **Implementation guidance:** Define **contact with special interest groups** through its owner, scope, trigger, workflow, exceptions, and evidence.
- **Best practice:** Embed it in normal work, test operation and effectiveness, and review it after material change or failure.

## Practical example

The security lead joins a sector information-sharing group, routes relevant alerts into the threat-review process, and records which alerts changed monitoring or treatment while withholding customer and incident details not approved for sharing.

## Evidence to retain

- approved policy, standard, or procedure
- owner and responsibility record
- operating records from the relevant workflow
- exception and risk-acceptance records
- control test or audit evidence

## Common mistakes

- Membership in groups is informal — one person follows a mailing list with no mechanism to share insights internally.
- No confidentiality rules exist for information sharing — sensitive organizational details are disclosed in public forums.
- Participation is reactive — the organization only engages after an incident rather than building relationships proactively.
- Groups are joined but intelligence is not operationalized — alerts are received but not triaged, analyzed, or acted upon.

## Auditor questions

- Which special interest groups or professional associations does the organization participate in?
- How is intelligence from these groups captured, assessed, and distributed internally?
- What rules govern the sharing of organizational information with external groups?
- Show an example of a security improvement that resulted from external group participation.

## Checklist

- [ ] relevant special interest groups identified and joined
- [ ] designated contacts for each group assigned
- [ ] information-sharing rules defined
- [ ] intelligence from groups routed to relevant internal teams
- [ ] participation reviewed for value and relevance periodically
- [ ] confidentiality safeguards in place for shared information
