# A.6.6 Confidentiality or non-disclosure agreements

## Overview

Confidentiality and non-disclosure agreements define which information must be protected, permitted use, disclosure conditions, duration, return or destruction, and consequences. Terms should match applicable law and the relationship's actual risk.

## Purpose

The purpose of A.6.6 is to reduce the likelihood or impact of failures related to **confidentiality or non-disclosure agreements**. A well-designed control makes the expected outcome, accountability, operating trigger, exception path, and assurance method clear enough to be repeated and tested.

## ISO requirement, implementation guidance, and best practice

- **ISO requirement:** Annex A controls are not automatically mandatory. Determine necessary controls through risk treatment, record applicability in the Statement of Applicability, and implement A.6.6 when selected or otherwise committed to.
- **Implementation guidance:** Define **confidentiality or non-disclosure agreements** through its owner, scope, trigger, workflow, exceptions, and evidence.
- **Best practice:** Embed it in normal work, test operation and effectiveness, and review it after material change or failure.

## Key concepts

- **Applicability:** why the control is or is not needed in context.
- **Control owner:** the role accountable for design, operation, evidence, and improvement.
- **Operating evidence:** scoped, dated records showing what occurred and who approved it.
- **Effectiveness:** achievement of the intended outcome, not activity completion alone.

## Practical implementation

This control addresses the human and employment lifecycle of security. It should combine clear expectations, competence, fair enforcement, reporting channels, and timely changes when people join, change roles, or leave.

For A.6.6, begin by identifying the specific risk, legal requirement, contractual commitment, or operational need that makes the control necessary. The control owner should then define what is in scope, which roles perform the activity, which systems or data sources are authoritative, how exceptions are handled, and what evidence proves that the control operated.

## Practical example

A specialist supplier signs an agreement covering customer data and architecture before receiving design material. The owner reviews the terms when the scope expands and records return or secure deletion at contract end.

## Evidence to retain

- approved policy, standard, or procedure
- owner and responsibility record
- operating records from the relevant workflow
- exception and risk-acceptance records
- control test or audit evidence

Retain both design and operating evidence; policy alone does not prove operation. Prefer authoritative, scoped records with approvals, exceptions, and remediation.

## Common mistakes

- policy exists without reliable operation;
- ownership or scope is unclear;
- exceptions lack approval or expiry; and
- evidence or corrective action does not demonstrate effectiveness.

## Auditor questions

- Which risk or requirement does the control address?
- Who owns and operates it, and how is scope determined?
- Which evidence shows recent operation and exception handling?
- How is effectiveness tested and failure remediated?
- What changed after the latest significant review or event?

## Checklist

- [ ] Control owner assigned
- [ ] Applicability decision recorded in the SoA
- [ ] Related risks identified
- [ ] Implementation approach documented
- [ ] Evidence sources identified
- [ ] Review frequency defined
