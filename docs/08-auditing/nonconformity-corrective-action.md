# Nonconformity and Corrective Action

A nonconformity is failure to meet a requirement. Corrective action addresses the cause and prevents recurrence.

## Typical flow

1. Record finding.
2. Contain or correct immediate issue.
3. Analyze root cause.
4. Define corrective action.
5. Assign owner and due date.
6. Implement action.
7. Verify effectiveness.
8. Close with retained evidence.

## Detailed explanation

A nonconformity exists when a requirement is not met. Correction fixes the immediate issue. Corrective action removes the cause so that the issue is less likely to recur.

### Example

Audit finds that terminated users retained access. Removing the sampled accounts is correction. Root-cause analysis discovers that HR termination events do not reach several software as a service (SaaS) owners. Corrective action integrates notifications, assigns SaaS ownership, and introduces completion monitoring.

### Best practices

- state the unmet requirement
- record objective evidence
- correct immediate exposure
- analyze root cause
- consider similar areas
- assign action and due date
- update risk and SoA where relevant
- verify effectiveness after enough time
- reopen ineffective actions

### Common mistakes

Organizations rename remediation as root cause, blame individuals without examining process design, close findings when tasks are completed, and fail to test recurrence.

## Practical example

An internal auditor samples a recently completed activity, traces it from the governing requirement to the operating record, checks exceptions, and confirms that identified weaknesses resulted in effective corrective action.

## Evidence to retain

Retain records showing both design decisions and actual operation, such as:

- audit scope, criteria, and sampling rationale
- source evidence and interview notes
- findings and agreed actions
- follow-up evidence confirming effectiveness
